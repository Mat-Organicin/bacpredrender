Bacteriocin Motifs Reference

This page shows the motifs that are automatically highlighted throughout BacPred

About Motif Detection

BacPred automatically highlights important bacteriocin motifs in sequence text throughout the application. Hover over any highlighted pattern to see detailed information about what that motif represents.

The highlighting system recognizes both exact matches (like "YGNGV") and pattern-based motifs (like "CxxC" where "x" can be any amino acid).

Example:

MKFNLDLRKALKGFGRGASYC

Try hovering over the highlighted portions above!

Bacteriocin Motif Catalog

Class I: Lantibiotics

Leader Peptide FNLD Motif

Pattern: FNLD

A conserved segment at the C-terminus of the leader peptide that directs post‐translational modifications (via LanB/LanC enzymes) and proper cleavage before the mature peptide forms.

Example sequence containing this motif:

MSTKDFNLDLVSVSKKDSGASPR
Reference: Bierbaum & Sahl (2009)

Lanthionine Ring Formation Motif

Pattern: SxxxC

Represents a dehydrated serine (S) and a downstream cysteine (C) separated by ~3 variable residues; these pair to form a thioether (lanthionine) bridge that rigidifies the mature peptide and supports lipid II binding (e.g., nisin's ring A).

Example sequence containing this motif:

ITSISLCTPGCKTGALMGCNMKTATCHCSIHVSK
Reference: Bierbaum & Sahl (2009)

Hinge Region

Pattern: NMK

A short (~3-residue) flexible linker (commonly "NMK") connecting the lipid II-binding domain to the membrane-active segment, allowing the peptide to bend during pore formation.

Example sequence containing this motif:

ITSISLCTPGCKTGALMGCNMKTATCHCSIHVSK
Reference: Drider et al. (2006)

Class IIa: Pediocin-Like Bacteriocins

Pediocin Box – YGNGV Motif

Pattern: YGNGV

A highly conserved N-terminal sequence critical for binding the mannose-phosphotransferase (Man-PTS) receptor on target cells; it defines the pediocin-like family.

Example sequence containing this motif:

KYYGNGVTCGKHSCSVDWGKATTCIINNGAMAWATGGHQGNHKC
Reference: Drider et al. (2006)

Disulfide-Bridge Motif

Pattern: CxxC

Two cysteines (C) separated by ~2 variable residues form a disulfide bond that stabilizes the N-terminal β-sheet structure needed for correct receptor binding.

Example sequence containing this motif:

KYYGNGVTCGKHSCSVDWGKATTCIINNGAMAWATGGHQGNHKC
Reference: Drider et al. (2006)

C-Terminal Hydrophobic Helix/Tail

Pattern: xxALxxVLxx

An amphipathic stretch rich in hydrophobic residues (e.g., A, L, V) that folds into a helix, facilitating membrane insertion after receptor binding.

Example sequence containing this motif:

RTGKHALVLKGLAKFWALVLKA
Reference: Drider et al. (2006); Ekblad et al. (2016)

Class IIb: Two-Peptide Bacteriocins

GxxxG Helix–Helix Interaction Motif

Pattern: GxxxG

Two glycine (G) residues separated by any three amino acids; this "glycine zipper" promotes close helix–helix packing between complementary peptides that act together to form a membrane pore.

Example sequence containing this motif:

SRNAYNGIKAGIVGSAMAGATVGAVASLFGPHAGAVMGVKVSKH
Reference: Ekblad et al. (2016)

Class III: Bacteriolysins

Catalytic HxH Motif

Pattern: HxH

A motif featuring two histidine residues (with 0–2 intervening residues) that coordinate a Zn²⁺ ion in metallopeptidase bacteriolysins, essential for cell wall degradation.

Example sequence containing this motif:

MKKIAGLFALAATIFGAPVYAQETHYLNHVGKGAFTKDQPANMGLYERSDKDAGKLLYKMTRYGWNHQEPHMGKDL
Reference: Yuan et al. (2004); Bierbaum & Sahl (2009)

LysM Motif

Pattern: xxGxxDxx

A short sequence fragment from the LysM domain, where conserved glycine (G) and aspartate (D) residues contribute to peptidoglycan binding.

Example sequence containing this motif:

ITVGKNDGSVCDGSLRPYVEADGSILYTVGEFVLPNELTGQ
Reference: Cleveland et al. (2001)

SH3b Motif

Pattern: xxYxxPxx

A representative segment from the SH3b domain, with conserved tyrosine (Y) and proline (P) that help stabilize the beta-barrel fold for specific binding to cell wall components.

Example sequence containing this motif:

EIYFKYPTVGDYRMVIEGPYKDIPAFRKVEGSVNRFHTEIFDKYTGNK
Reference: Cleveland et al. (2001)
